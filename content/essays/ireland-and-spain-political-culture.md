## Abstract

Ireland and Spain are two European democracies that share several historical features: a strong Catholic tradition, long experiences of emigration, societies that remained socially conservative for much of the twentieth century, and intense processes of political, economic and cultural modernisation. Yet their contemporary political cultures cannot be understood through the same historical wounds. In Ireland, modern politics has been deeply shaped by British rule, the struggle for independence, the partition of the island and the complex relationship with the United Kingdom. In Spain, by contrast, contemporary political culture remains marked by the Civil War, Franco's dictatorship, the democratic transition, public memory and internal territorial tensions.

This essay compares both cases in order to analyse how historical experience continues to influence the way each society understands national identity, religion, the state, Europe and contemporary political conflict.

## Research question

How do Ireland's and Spain's historical experiences continue to shape the way both societies understand politics today?

## Introduction

Comparing Ireland and Spain is useful precisely because they appear to share certain historical similarities while following very different political paths. Both countries belong to Western Europe. Both have been strongly influenced by Catholicism. Both experienced significant emigration and both underwent major social transformation during the twentieth and early twenty-first centuries.

Nevertheless, the emotional and historical foundations of their political cultures are not the same. In Ireland, modern politics has been organised around questions of sovereignty, national independence, colonial memory, partition and the relationship with the United Kingdom. Irish political identity has often been defined in relation to an external power. In Spain, by contrast, the central fracture of contemporary politics comes from an internal wound: the Civil War, Franco's dictatorship and the later transition to democracy [11-16].

This distinction matters because political culture cannot be reduced to institutions, parties or elections. It also includes the narratives that a society builds about itself: what it remembers, what it prefers to leave unspoken, how it understands authority, what it considers legitimate, how it defines the nation and which conflicts remain unresolved. From this perspective, Ireland and Spain reveal two different relationships between history and politics. In the Irish case, colonial experience and independence continue to influence how sovereignty, identity and external relations are understood [1-4].

In the Spanish case, the memory of dictatorship, territorial debate and the democratic transition continue to shape discussions about the state, the nation and democracy [16-19].

## Different historical wounds

One of the most important differences between Ireland and Spain lies in the origin of their main contemporary political wounds. Although both countries share certain features - Catholic tradition, emigration, late modernisation and European integration - their current political cultures are built on very different historical experiences. In Ireland, modern political memory is largely organised around the relationship with an external power: the United Kingdom. In Spain, by contrast, the central fracture comes from an internal conflict: the Civil War, Franco's dictatorship and the way the country rebuilt its democracy after 1975.

In the Irish case, modern political history was shaped by centuries of British rule. The relationship between Ireland and Great Britain was not only administrative or territorial; it was also cultural, religious, economic and symbolic. Modern Irish identity developed partly in opposition to a power perceived as external, Protestant, imperial and British. Against this background, Catholicism, rural memory, the Irish language, nationalist literature and the claim to sovereignty became important elements in imagining a distinct political community [1-4].

This opposition gave Irish politics a strong connection to national independence. Political struggle was not understood only as competition between parties, but as a question of self-determination: who had the right to govern Ireland, where power should be exercised from, and what it meant to be Irish. For that reason, Irish independence cannot be interpreted only as an institutional change. It was also a symbolic transformation: the affirmation of a distinct national identity after a long experience of political subordination [3].

However, independence did not fully close the historical wound. Partition left a division that continued to shape Irish politics throughout the twentieth century. On one side, an independent Irish state was consolidated; on the other, Northern Ireland remained within the United Kingdom. This division was not merely geographical.

It also reflected religious, political and identity differences between nationalist communities, largely Catholic and supportive of Irish unity, and unionist communities, largely Protestant and attached to remaining within the United Kingdom [4,8,9]. The existence of Northern Ireland turned national identity into a permanently open question. The conflict known as the Troubles showed how colonial history, religion, identity and sovereignty could continue to have deep political and social consequences. Although the peace agreements allowed a new stage to emerge, the question of the border, the relationship with the United Kingdom and the possibility of future reunification remain present in Irish political imagination, particularly after Brexit [8,9].

For this reason, contemporary Irish politics still carries a visible historical dimension. Discussing sovereignty, neutrality, the European Union, the border or relations with the United Kingdom inevitably means engaging with the past. Even when current debates are economic or social - housing, cost of living, migration or foreign investment - Irish political culture remains marked by a memory of independence, national vulnerability and assertion in relation to a much larger neighbouring power. Spain followed a different path.

Its main contemporary political wound does not come from opposition to an external power, but from an internal fracture. The Spanish Civil War was not simply a military conflict: it represented the extreme expression of social, ideological, religious, territorial and economic tensions that had been accumulating for decades. It brought into conflict incompatible visions of the country: monarchy or republic, centralism or territorial plurality, religious tradition or secularisation, social revolution or preservation of order, authoritarianism or democracy [11,12]. The Francoist victory did not close that fracture; it transformed it into a dictatorship that lasted almost four decades.

During Franco's regime, the state imposed a centralist, authoritarian, national-Catholic and deeply anti-communist idea of Spain. Political plurality was repressed, peripheral languages and cultures were limited or excluded from public life, and the memory of the defeated was removed from the official narrative. The dictatorship did not simply govern the country: it also attempted to define what it meant to be Spanish [13-15]. This is a crucial difference from the Irish case.

In Ireland, modern national identity was built in relation to external domination. In Spain, by contrast, the conflict took place within the state itself and divided Spaniards against Spaniards. For this reason, Spanish political memory tends to be more strongly marked by internal division, ideological polarisation and the difficulty of building a shared account of the recent past. The Spanish democratic transition restored freedoms, legalised parties, approved a constitution and created a decentralised political system based on autonomous communities [18,19].

From an institutional perspective, it was a decisive process for integrating Spain into the framework of European democracies. However, it also had limits. In order to guarantee stability, the transition relied on a logic of consensus that, in many cases, avoided a deep public confrontation with the memory of repression, the responsibilities of Francoism or the recognition of victims [16,21]. This is why the Spanish transition continues to be interpreted in different ways.

For some, it represents a historical success: the peaceful move from dictatorship to parliamentary democracy, supported by broad agreements and a desire for reconciliation. For others, it was an incomplete democratisation, conditioned by structures inherited from Francoism and by a political culture that often preferred silence to open conflict over memory. This ambivalence helps explain why issues such as historical memory, the monarchy, Francoism, the role of the Church and the legitimacy of the territorial model still generate intense debate. The Spanish territorial model is another key element.

The 1978 Constitution created a state of autonomous communities that recognised territorial diversity, but it did not completely resolve the tension between national unity and internal plurality. Catalonia, the Basque Country and Galicia, among other territories, preserve strong national, linguistic and cultural identities. In this sense, Spain does not only debate its authoritarian past; it also debates the very definition of the nation: whether it should be understood as a single political nation or as a state containing several national and cultural identities [17-19,24]. In comparison, Ireland also has an open territorial question, but of a different nature.

The Irish division is linked to an international border and to the relationship with the United Kingdom. The Spanish territorial tension, by contrast, develops within the democratic state itself. This produces two different forms of political conflict: in Ireland, the historical question revolves around independence, partition and the relationship with a neighbouring power; in Spain, it revolves around internal memory, decentralisation, peripheral nationalisms and the legitimacy of the model created after the transition. These historical wounds do not automatically determine present-day politics, but they do condition the language through which each society interprets conflict.

Ireland often reads political debates through the experience of sovereignty, independence and the relationship with the United Kingdom. Spain often interprets its conflicts through the memory of dictatorship, ideological polarisation and the tension between national unity and territorial diversity. Therefore, the difference between both countries is not only institutional. It lies in the kind of past that still weighs on their public life.

Ireland often looks towards a history of external domination and national assertion. Spain looks towards a history of internal fracture, democratic reconstruction and dispute over the meaning of the state itself. In both cases, the past does not disappear: it becomes political memory, public symbols, recurring debates and different ways of understanding democracy.

## Religion and political culture

> The same religion did not play the same political role: in Ireland, Catholicism often marked national difference; in Spain, it became more closely connected to state power.

Religion is one of the elements that can both bring Ireland and Spain closer together and distinguish them sharply. Both countries have been historically Catholic, and for a long time Catholicism had a deep influence on social, family, educational and moral life. However, the political function of Catholicism was not exactly the same in both cases.

In Ireland, religion became part of a national identity constructed in opposition to British and Protestant rule. In Spain, by contrast, Catholicism was more directly linked to the official idea of the nation, to state power and, above all, to the ideological project of Francoism [7,15]. In Ireland, Catholicism was for centuries much more than a private religion. It functioned as a boundary of identity.

In a context shaped by British rule, the difference between Catholics and Protestants was not only a matter of belief, but also of power, belonging and social position. The Catholic majority identified with a politically subordinated community, while Protestantism was often associated with the Anglo-Irish elites, imperial authority and British power. Therefore, being Catholic in Ireland did not simply mean belonging to a religious confession; it could also mean belonging to a distinct national community [6,7]. This relationship between Catholicism and national identity gave the Catholic Church a very strong social role in the Irish state after independence.

The Church influenced not only public morality, but also education, family life, sexuality, social assistance and the definition of respectable society. In a country that had recently affirmed itself as an independent nation, Catholicism helped construct an image of Ireland as morally distinct from Great Britain. For a long time, the Irish nation was imagined as Catholic, rural, family-centred, conservative and culturally different from the British Protestant world [7]. Yet this religious centrality also produced intense forms of social control.

The Catholic Church had a considerable capacity to define what was acceptable and unacceptable in private life. Issues such as divorce, contraception, abortion, female sexuality, motherhood outside marriage and education were deeply conditioned by Catholic morality. In this sense, religion acted as an element of national cohesion, but also as a mechanism of social discipline. Ireland's transformation in recent decades has been particularly striking because it represented a rapid break with that model.

As Ireland modernised, urbanised, opened economically and became more integrated into Europe, the social authority of the Church weakened. Abuse scandals, generational change, the increasing public role of women, cultural globalisation and economic growth all contributed to reducing the Church's ability to define collective morality. Referendums on same-sex marriage and abortion symbolised a profound change: Ireland moved from being seen as a conservative Catholic society to projecting a much more secular, liberal and socially transformed image. In Spain, Catholicism also had enormous influence, but its political role followed a different trajectory.

For centuries, Spanish identity had been deeply connected to Catholicism, from confessional monarchy to the idea of religious unity as part of national imagination. In the twentieth century, however, the link between religion and politics became particularly intense during the Civil War and Franco's dictatorship. Francoism presented Catholicism as one of the pillars of the Spanish nation and built a national-Catholic project in which religion, homeland, authority and social order were closely connected [13-15]. During the dictatorship, the Catholic Church held a privileged position in education, public morality and the symbolic legitimation of the regime.

Francoism did not merely allow religious influence; it incorporated it into its political identity. The idea of Spain defended by the regime was centralist, anti-communist, traditional, hierarchical and Catholic. In this context, Catholicism did not function mainly as resistance to an external power, as in the Irish case, but as part of the official discourse of the state. This makes religious memory in Spain more ambivalent.

For part of society, Catholicism belongs to cultural, family and community tradition. For others, it is linked to moral repression, authoritarianism, censorship, gender inequality and the legitimation of Francoism. For that reason, debates about religion in Spain are not only spiritual or moral; they are also debates about historical memory, education, institutional power and the model of society. The democratic transition profoundly changed the relationship between Church and state.

The 1978 Constitution established a non-confessional state, recognised religious freedom and avoided the official identification of the state with a single confession [18,19]. However, Spanish secularisation did not mean the complete disappearance of Catholic influence. The Church continued to be present in areas such as state-funded private education, religious teaching, debates on abortion, marriage, family, historical memory and public funding. In Spain, therefore, Catholicism ceased to be the official religion of the state, but it did not disappear as a cultural and political actor.

The comparison with Ireland reveals an important distinction. In Ireland, Catholicism was for a long time a form of cultural resistance and national differentiation in relation to British and Protestant power. After independence, that same religion became a dominant force within Irish society, until recent secularisation sharply reduced its public authority. In Spain, by contrast, Catholicism was more closely associated with state power, particularly under Francoism, and its public presence therefore remains connected to debates about memory, institutional privilege and authoritarian inheritance.

Both countries have experienced secularisation, but not in the same way or with the same political meaning. In Ireland, recent secularisation can partly be understood as a break with a Church that had intensely regulated the social life of an independent nation. In Spain, secularisation is also connected to democratisation, social modernisation and distance from Francoist national Catholicism. In both cases, reducing the traditional weight of religion did not simply mean becoming less religious; it meant redefining the relationship between private morality, individual rights, public authority and national identity.

Religion, therefore, helps explain how each country has understood modernity. In Ireland, social modernisation involved questioning a national identity that had long been imagined as deeply Catholic. In Spain, democratic modernisation involved separating the idea of the nation from Catholicism's moral monopoly and creating space for a more plural, secular and territorially diverse society. In conclusion, Ireland and Spain show two distinct relationships between Catholicism and politics.

In Ireland, religion was first an identity boundary in relation to British power and later an internal social authority that contemporary society has increasingly questioned. In Spain, Catholicism was for a long time part of the official narrative of the state and, especially during Franco's dictatorship, one of the pillars of an authoritarian idea of nation. In both cases, secularisation does not erase the religious past; it transforms its political meaning.

## National identity and territory

> Ireland asks how to close a nation divided by a border; Spain asks how to integrate several territorial identities within a single state.

National identity is another area in which Ireland and Spain reveal deep differences. In both countries, the nation is not merely an administrative reality, but a historical construction shaped by symbols, memory, language, religion, territory and conflict. However, the national question is formulated differently in each case.

In Ireland, national identity has largely been constructed in relation to an external power and around a divided island. In Spain, national identity is debated within the state itself and is linked to the coexistence of different territorial, linguistic and cultural identities. In Ireland, modern national identity developed in opposition to British rule. This does not mean that Irish identity emerged only as a reaction to the United Kingdom, but the colonial experience gave national politics a very specific shape.

Language, Catholicism, rural memory, literature, music and the claim to sovereignty all helped imagine a community distinct from the British one. The Irish nation was not merely a territory; it was a way of affirming political and cultural existence in relation to an authority perceived as external [1-4]. Independence turned that identity into a state project. However, partition prevented cultural nation, political nation and territory from fully coinciding.

The independent Irish state did not include the entire island. Northern Ireland remained within the United Kingdom, keeping open the question of the limits of the Irish nation. Is Ireland the state that exists politically? Is it the entire island?

Is it a broader cultural community that exceeds the border? These questions are not only historical; they continue to have political consequences. Partition turned the border into a symbol. For some, it represented an artificial division inherited from British rule.

For others, especially unionist communities in Northern Ireland, it guaranteed a British and Protestant identity that did not wish to be absorbed into a Catholic-majority Irish state. Territory therefore stopped being simply a geographical matter. It became an expression of belonging, fear, memory and legitimacy [8,9]. The Northern Irish conflict showed how closely identity and territory can be intertwined.

In Northern Ireland, the division between nationalists and unionists could not be reduced only to religion, even though religion functioned as a community marker. The conflict involved two different ways of understanding sovereignty and belonging: one linked to Irish unity and another linked to continued membership of the United Kingdom. For decades, politics was organised around a question that appeared simple but was historically charged: to which political community does this territory belong? In this sense, Ireland maintains an open national question, but one largely located at the border between two states and in the relationship with the United Kingdom.

The Republic of Ireland has built its own national identity, yet the existence of Northern Ireland prevents the national narrative from being completely closed. Brexit reactivated this issue because it gave renewed political relevance to the border. A border that the peace process had tried to make almost invisible reappeared as an institutional, economic and symbolic problem. Spain presents a different situation.

Territorial tension is not primarily organised against an external power, but within the state itself. Spanish identity has historically coexisted with strong national, cultural and linguistic identities in territories such as Catalonia, the Basque Country and Galicia. This turns the Spanish national question into an internal one: it is not only about defining Spain in relation to another country, but about deciding how different forms of belonging can coexist within the same state [17-19]. During Franco's dictatorship, the state imposed a centralist and uniform vision of the Spanish nation.

Linguistic, cultural and political diversity was subordinated to an official idea of Spain as indivisible, Catholic and predominantly Castilian in its public expression. This imposition did not eliminate territorial identities, but it repressed or limited them in public and institutional life. Therefore, after Franco's death, democratic recovery was closely linked to the recovery of territorial plurality. The 1978 Constitution attempted to address this tension through the state of autonomous communities.

This model recognised territorial diversity and granted broad powers to the autonomous communities. It was a formula of balance: it did not define Spain as a federal state, but it did not preserve Francoist centralism either. It aimed to integrate different territorial demands within a shared constitutional framework. However, this solution also left ambiguities [18,19,24].

One of these ambiguities lies in the idea of nation itself. For part of Spanish society, Spain is a single nation composed of regions with distinct cultural characteristics. For other sectors, especially in Catalonia, the Basque Country or Galicia, Spain is a plurinational state in which several national identities coexist. This difference is not merely terminological.

It affects the way sovereignty, language, education, funding, memory and institutional legitimacy are understood [17]. The Catalan case has made the strength of this conflict visible. Catalan independence cannot be understood only as an economic or institutional dispute. It also expresses deeper tensions concerning recognition, identity, self-government and belonging.

For its supporters, Catalonia constitutes a national community with the right to decide its political future. For its critics, independence challenges shared sovereignty and threatens equality among citizens. Behind the debate lie two different conceptions of the state: one more centralised and another more plurinational. The Basque Country has a different trajectory, marked by its own national identity, a distinct language, a strong fiscal self-government and the memory of ETA's violence.

In this case, the territorial debate is also shaped by the history of political and armed conflict, which adds a specific dimension to the relationship between identity, violence, democracy and coexistence. Galicia, although with lower political intensity in terms of independence, also preserves a cultural and linguistic identity that forms part of the wider debate on the internal plurality of the state. Compared with Ireland, Spain has a more plural and decentralised territorial question. In Ireland, the national problem is closely linked to a concrete partition and to the relationship with the United Kingdom.

In Spain, the territorial debate takes multiple forms and is expressed within a state composed of autonomous communities with different levels of identity, political power and national claim. The question is not only where the border lies, but what it means to belong to Spain and how many legitimate forms of belonging can fit within the state. This difference produces different political cultures. In Ireland, national identity tends to be linked to the memory of a struggle for independence and to the relationship with an external power.

In Spain, national identity is more closely linked to the uneasy coexistence between unity and internal diversity. For that reason, national symbols, languages, flags and territorial debates carry different emotional weight in each country. The Irish national narrative has benefited, in part, from a relatively clear symbolic framework: a small nation that fought for independence from a larger empire. Although this narrative simplifies a complex historical reality, it offers a strong shared frame.

Spain, by contrast, has more difficulty constructing a shared national narrative because its recent history is marked by a civil war, a centralist dictatorship and a transition that tried to integrate differences without completely resolving disagreement over the idea of nation itself. This does not mean that Ireland has a simple identity or that Spain is exceptionally conflictual. Both countries contain internal contradictions. Ireland has also had to critically reconsider its national story, its relationship with the Church, its treatment of women, its emigration and its role in a globalised economy.

Spain has also built a decentralised democracy, integrated into Europe and capable of recognising much of its internal diversity. What differs is the kind of national tension that runs through each country. In summary, Ireland and Spain show two different relationships between identity and territory. In Ireland, the national question is marked by colonial experience, independence, partition and the border with the United Kingdom.

In Spain, the national question is expressed through the tension between state unity and internal territorial plurality. Both cases show that the nation is not only a political category; it is a way of organising memory, belonging and power. Europe as modernisation For Ireland, Europe was a way of gaining room for manoeuvre in relation to the United Kingdom; for Spain, it was a way of confirming its democratic return after dictatorship. After considering historical wounds, religion and national identity, the relationship with Europe reveals another key dimension of Ireland and Spain: the need to modernise and redefine themselves within a broader political framework.

In both countries, European integration was not only an economic or institutional matter. It also had symbolic value. Europe represented openness, stability, modernisation and belonging to a shared democratic space. However, Europe did not mean exactly the same thing for Ireland and Spain.

In Ireland, membership of the European Communities helped reduce historical dependence on the United Kingdom and allowed the country to integrate into a broader international space. In Spain, entry into Europe signified the consolidation of democracy after decades of dictatorship and political isolation. For both countries, Europe functioned as a promise of the future, but it responded to different historical needs [10,20,22,23]. Ireland joined the European Communities in 1973, together with the United Kingdom and Denmark [10].

This is important because it reveals the ambivalent nature of Ireland's relationship with its former dominant power. For a long time, the Irish economy had been closely linked to the British economy, through trade, emigration and structural dependence. European membership allowed Ireland to broaden its political and economic horizon. It was no longer only a small nation beside the United Kingdom; it could present itself as a European state with its own voice within a supranational community.

From this perspective, Europe helped Ireland strengthen its sovereignty in a paradoxical way. By joining a supranational project, Ireland did not simply lose autonomy; it gained room to manoeuvre in relation to British influence. The European Union offered market access, funds, institutional networks and international recognition. It also allowed Irish identity to be situated within a wider framework: Ireland could be small, but within Europe it became more visible and less dependent on its bilateral relationship with the United Kingdom.

Irish modernisation in the late twentieth and early twenty-first centuries cannot be understood without this European context. Economic openness, foreign investment, improved infrastructure, the growth of the technology sector and Dublin's transformation into an international capital were part of a broader change. Ireland moved from being long perceived as a peripheral, poorer and emigrant country to becoming an open, globalised and highly integrated European economy [1,10]. This transformation had cultural and political consequences.

Contemporary Ireland began to move away from the traditional image of a rural, Catholic and conservative country. Economic growth, urbanisation, immigration, higher education and exposure to European and global values contributed to deep social change. The European framework was not the only cause of this evolution, but it provided a context of openness and modernisation that facilitated it. In the Spanish case, Europe had a different meaning.

Spain did not join the European Communities until 1986, after Franco's death, the democratic transition and the approval of the 1978 Constitution [20]. European integration was therefore directly associated with the consolidation of democracy. Entering Europe did not mean only accessing a common market; it meant confirming that Spain had left dictatorship behind and returned to the Western democratic political space. During Francoism, Spain had an ambivalent relationship with Europe.

Geographically, it was European, but politically it was distant from the democratic values that became consolidated in Western Europe after the Second World War. Although Franco's dictatorship was partially accepted in the context of the Cold War because of its anti-communism, it did not fully fit the European project based on parliamentary democracy, rights and supranational cooperation. For democratic Spain, Europe therefore functioned as a horizon of normalisation [20,22]. Entry into the European Communities was experienced as a form of international legitimacy.

Spain was not only modernising economically; it was also joining a political identity from which it had been excluded for decades. Europe symbolised democracy, development, cultural openness, rights, infrastructure and future. In the political imagination of the transition and the following years, 'being European' meant overcoming backwardness, isolation and authoritarian exceptionalism [22]. European influence in Spain was significant.

European funds contributed to modernising infrastructure, developing regions, transforming productive sectors and bringing the country closer to the economic standards of its environment. But the impact was not only material. Europe also provided a framework of institutional stability. European integration helped consolidate Spanish democracy and placed its internal conflicts within a broader political space.

Nevertheless, Spain's relationship with Europe also has a complex dimension. For years, Europe was seen as a guarantee of modernity and progress, almost as an external solution to the country's historical problems. This view had symbolic force, but it could also hide unresolved internal conflicts: territorial inequalities, identity tensions, debates about historical memory, economic crises and distrust of institutions. Europe helped modernise Spain, but it did not erase the fractures of Spanish political culture.

The comparison with Ireland shows two different forms of Europeanisation. In Ireland, Europe served largely to expand the practical sovereignty of a small country and reduce its historical dependence on the United Kingdom. In Spain, Europe served to consolidate democracy and legitimise the transition from dictatorship to a modern state integrated into the West. In both cases, the European Union acted as a structure of opportunity: it offered resources, recognition and a narrative of the future.

There is also a difference in the emotional relationship each country has with Europe. For Ireland, Europe has been a way of reinforcing autonomy in a context shaped by British proximity. This became especially visible with Brexit. The United Kingdom's withdrawal from the European Union reinforced Ireland's position as a committed member state and turned the Irish border into a central issue in international negotiation.

In that moment, Europe was not only a market or an institution; it was also a political guarantee against the consequences of a British decision. For Spain, Europe continues to function as a reference point for modernisation and comparison. Debates about democratic quality, the economy, employment, housing, financing, social rights or territorial management are often measured against 'Europe'. In Spanish political language, Europe frequently appears as a standard, aspiration or external pressure.

This shows how deeply European integration became part of the imagination of democratic modernisation. Despite their differences, both countries share one idea: Europe allowed them to rewrite part of their place in the world. Ireland could present itself as a small but global, European and open nation. Spain could present itself as a modern democracy, integrated and distant from Francoist isolation.

In both cases, Europe worked as a way of overcoming historical limitations: external dependence in the Irish case, authoritarian isolation in the Spanish case. Europe did not, however, replace national identities or erase historical memories. Rather, it reorganised them. Ireland remained Irish, but within a European network that gave it new tools in relation to the United Kingdom.

Spain continued to debate its memory, territorial model and national identity, but within a European framework that shaped its political and economic possibilities. Europeanisation did not remove the past; it placed it within a new structure. In conclusion, the European Union acted as a force of modernisation in both countries, but with different meanings. For Ireland, it was a path of openness and autonomy in relation to historical dependence.

For Spain, it was a path of democratic legitimacy and reintegration after dictatorship. In both cases, Europe offered a future, but each country arrived at that future from different wounds.

## Contemporary political culture

Historical differences between Ireland and Spain do not belong only to the past. They continue to appear in the way both societies understand politics, discuss conflicts and build collective priorities. The current political culture of each country is formed by institutions, parties and contemporary debates, but also by memories, symbols and wounds that have not completely disappeared. In Ireland, contemporary politics is often perceived as more pragmatic and less ideologically polarised than Spanish politics, although this does not mean that it lacks deep conflicts.

Irish divisions are not always expressed in classical left-right terms, but through issues such as housing, cost of living, public services, the relationship with the United Kingdom, national identity, neutrality, immigration and the future of Northern Ireland [1,4]. The history of independence and partition remains present in the Irish political system. Even the party structure preserves traces of the Irish Civil War and the debates surrounding the Anglo- Irish Treaty. For much of the twentieth century, Irish politics was dominated by Fianna Fail and Fine Gael, two parties whose original distinction was not based primarily on a conventional left- right ideological divide, but on historical positions concerning the treaty, independence and the legitimacy of the new state.

Although those differences have changed and softened, they show how national history left lasting marks on political organisation [1,3,4]. The relationship with Northern Ireland remains one of the most sensitive issues in Irish politics. The Good Friday Agreement created a framework for peace and cooperation, but it did not eliminate the question of reunification or the differences between communities [8,9]. Brexit placed this question again at the centre of debate.

The United Kingdom's exit from the European Union created a particular tension on the island because it threatened to make the border between Northern Ireland and the Republic of Ireland visible again. This demonstrated how strongly territorial and colonial histories still condition contemporary political decisions. At the same time, Ireland has changed profoundly in social terms. In a few decades, it moved from being perceived as a conservative society strongly influenced by the Catholic Church to becoming a country associated with rapid social transformation.

The approval of same-sex marriage by referendum, the liberalisation of abortion and the weakening of ecclesiastical authority symbolise a new political culture that is more secular, urban and liberal in many respects. These changes do not eliminate the weight of the past, but they show a society that has redefined its public values with remarkable speed. However, Irish modernisation has also created new tensions. Economic success, the attraction of large multinational companies and Dublin's transformation into a globalised capital coexist with serious problems of housing, inequality, pressure on public services and cost of living.

This has changed the country's political language. While for a long time the main questions revolved around independence, religion and sovereignty, today many concerns focus on the state's capacity to guarantee a decent life in a globalised economy. Spain presents a different political culture, more strongly marked by ideological polarisation, historical memory and the internal territorial question. Contemporary Spanish politics cannot be understood without the Civil War, Francoism, the democratic transition and the autonomous model [11-19].

Although decades have passed since Franco's death, debates about memory, symbols, victims, monarchy, the Constitution, the Church, centralism and nationalisms continue to reappear. One important feature of Spanish political culture is the difficulty of separating the present from the recent past. Many current political discussions are interpreted through inherited categories: left and right, anti-Francoism and Francoism, centralism and autonomy, monarchy and republic, national unity and plurinationality. This does not mean that Spain is trapped in the past, but it does mean that the past continues to provide an emotional and symbolic framework for understanding present conflicts.

The democratic transition occupies a particularly complex position. For part of society, it represents the major political success of contemporary Spain: the ability to move from dictatorship to democracy through agreements, moderation and consensus. For another part, it also represents a source of limits: a process that stabilised the country but did not sufficiently address the memory of victims, the clarification of responsibilities or the continuity of certain institutional structures [16,21]. This double reading explains why the 1978 Constitution can be seen both as a symbol of democratic freedom and as a framework that some consider incomplete or insufficient.

The territorial question is another central axis. In Spain, politics does not debate only public policies, but also the definition of the state itself. Catalonia, the Basque Country and Galicia, among other territories, introduce a constant tension between national unity and internal diversity. The rise of Catalan independence in the last decade showed how strongly the autonomous model remains disputed.

For some, the state of autonomous communities has recognised territorial plurality and guaranteed self-government. For others, it has not resolved the existence of distinct national identities within Spain [17-19,24]. This territorial tension also feeds polarisation. Debates about Catalonia, the Basque Country, language, regional financing and historical memory tend to activate intense political identities.

Unlike Ireland, where the national question is linked mainly to an external border and the relationship with the United Kingdom, in Spain the national question runs through the interior of the state itself. This means that territorial disagreement mixes with party competition, ideological identity and interpretation of the past. Another important element of Spanish political culture is the relationship with authority and institutions. The experience of dictatorship and transition generated a democracy that values stability, but also a citizenship often distrustful of political elites.

Corruption, economic crises, austerity, youth unemployment and the perception of distance between institutions and everyday life have strengthened this distrust at different moments. This helps explain the emergence of new parties, the fragmentation of the political system and the weakening of the traditional two- party system. Compared with Spain, Ireland appears to have a political culture less centred on internal ideological memory and more oriented towards pragmatic problem-solving, although it also faces significant tensions. In Ireland, debates about housing, services, immigration and inequality occur in a society that has changed very quickly and is trying to balance economic growth, social cohesion and national identity.

In Spain, similar material problems exist, but they are inserted into a political culture that is more polarised and more heavily loaded with internal historical references. This difference should not be interpreted as the superiority of one model over the other. Ireland also faces deep conflicts: housing, inequality, dependence on multinationals, urban pressure, the relationship with Northern Ireland and identity debates. Spain has also built a solid, decentralised and socially plural democracy.

What matters is observing that each country organises its conflicts through different historical languages. In Ireland, the underlying political language is often marked by sovereignty, independence, the border and the relationship with the United Kingdom. In Spain, the underlying political language is often marked by the memory of dictatorship, the transition, ideological polarisation and the tension between unity and territorial plurality. Therefore, even when both countries discuss similar problems - housing, inequality, modernisation, social rights or European integration - they do not always interpret them through the same symbolic frameworks.

The contemporary political cultures of both countries show that history does not act simply as a chronology of past events. It functions as a reserve of symbols, fears, legitimacies and narratives. Every society inherits a way of naming its conflicts. Ireland and Spain may share European democratic institutions, but they do not share exactly the same historical vocabulary for speaking about nation, authority, memory or the future.

## Comparative analysis: two ways of understanding politics

> Ireland and Spain share the European democratic framework, but they do not share exactly the same political past. For this reason, they do not interpret questions of nation, authority, memory and future in the same way.

The comparison between Ireland and Spain reveals one central idea: two countries may share broad historical features - Catholic influence, emigration, European modernisation and recent social transformation - while developing very different political cultures. The difference lies not only in their institutions, but in the kind of historical experience that continues to organise public debate.

In Ireland, modern politics has largely been built around sovereignty, independence and the relationship with the United Kingdom. In Spain, contemporary politics remains closely connected to the memory of the Civil War, dictatorship, democratic transition and the tension between state unity and territorial plurality. Therefore, even when both countries debate similar issues - housing, inequality, social rights, national identity or the relationship with Europe - they do not always do so through the same historical language. A first major difference is the origin of the political fracture.

In Ireland, the main wound comes from a history of external domination, independence struggle and territorial partition. Modern Irish politics developed around a question of sovereignty: who had the right to govern Ireland and what relationship the island should have with the United Kingdom. Even after independence, partition kept the national question open, especially around Northern Ireland. In Spain, the central fracture comes from internal conflict.

The Civil War and Franco's dictatorship did not set Spain against an external power; they confronted incompatible projects within the country itself. This produced a political culture in which memory, democratic legitimacy, state authority and the interpretation of the recent past continue to carry significant weight. Spanish political disagreement is not only about specific policies; it is also about how the twentieth century should be remembered and what meaning should be given to the democracy born after the transition. This difference shapes how the nation is understood.

In Ireland, national identity has largely been constructed in relation to a neighbouring power and around a divided island. Although Irish reality is complex and cannot be reduced to a simple narrative, there is a strong national story based on independence, cultural resistance and assertion of sovereignty. Partition introduces a permanent tension, but it also offers a clear axis: the relationship between Ireland, Northern Ireland and the United Kingdom. In Spain, national identity is more contested within the state itself.

The tension is not located primarily at an external border, but in the coexistence of different territorial, linguistic and cultural identities. The 1978 Constitution created a state of autonomous communities in order to integrate this diversity, but it did not completely end the debate over whether Spain should be understood as a single political nation or as a plurinational state. This ambiguity forms part of contemporary Spanish political culture. Religion offers another relevant comparison.

In both countries, Catholicism had deep historical influence, but it fulfilled different political functions. In Ireland, Catholicism long operated as a marker of national identity in relation to British and Protestant power. In Spain, Catholicism was more closely connected to state power, especially during Franco's regime, when national Catholicism formed part of the official discourse. As a result, secularisation also has different meanings: in Ireland, it partly represents a break with a Church that organised the social life of an independent nation; in Spain, it also represents distance from a religion historically associated with state power and dictatorship.

Europe acted as an element of modernisation in both cases, but it did not have exactly the same meaning. For Ireland, European integration allowed the country to expand its economic and political space beyond its historical relationship with the United Kingdom. Europe offered an international structure in which Ireland could reinforce its practical sovereignty and reduce dependence on its neighbour. For Spain, Europe represented the consolidation of democracy after Francoism and the confirmation of its return to the Western political space.

In both countries, Europe symbolised future, openness and modernisation, but each arrived at that framework from different historical needs. The management of memory also differs. In Ireland, political memory is closely related to independence, partition and the Northern Irish conflict. The Good Friday Agreement represented an attempt to institutionalise coexistence and reduce violence through recognition, cooperation and balance between communities.

Although it did not eliminate all disagreements, it created a political framework for managing a very deep historical division [8,9]. In Spain, political memory is more centred on the Civil War, dictatorship and transition. The democratic transition prioritised institutional consensus and stability, but it left open the discussion about how to remember victims, how to interpret Francoism and how to evaluate the process of democratisation itself. For some, the transition represents a historical success; for others, it contains silences and limits that continue to influence the present.

This plurality of interpretations explains why historical memory remains politically sensitive [16,21]. The comparison also shows different forms of polarisation. Ireland is not free from tension, but its political system has historically been less organised around a conventional left-right ideological divide. For decades, the differences between its main parties were more connected to the history of the state and the Anglo-Irish Treaty than to a classical doctrinal division.

In Spain, by contrast, political competition has been more strongly shaped by ideological, territorial and memory-related divisions. This does not mean that Ireland is simply pragmatic or that Spain is only polarised, but it does mean that conflicts are structured differently. In general terms, Ireland tends to interpret many debates through a logic of sovereignty, independence, border and relationship with the United Kingdom. Spain tends to interpret many of its debates through a logic of internal memory, territorial model, democratic transition and state legitimacy.

This difference does not automatically determine citizens' political positions, but it influences the kinds of questions that return repeatedly to public debate. For that reason, the comparison between Ireland and Spain should not be framed as an opposition between a more modern country and a more backward one, or between a better and worse political culture. Both countries have experienced modernisation, democratisation, secularisation and European integration. Both have unresolved conflicts and significant achievements.

The relevant issue is different: each society organises its debates from a distinct historical memory. Ireland shows how an earlier colonial experience, partial independence and a disputed border can continue to influence the political culture of a contemporary European democracy. Spain shows how a civil war, a long dictatorship and a negotiated transition can continue to shape debates about memory, territory and identity. In both cases, history does not provide a single explanation, but it offers an interpretative framework that gives meaning to present conflicts.

Ultimately, the comparison reveals that politics is not understood only through laws, parties or elections. It is also understood through historical narratives. Each country inherits a way of naming its wounds, fears and aspirations. Ireland and Spain share the European democratic framework, but they do not share exactly the same political past.

That is why they do not interpret questions of nation, authority, memory and future in the same way.

## Final reflection

Comparing Ireland and Spain allows us to see how history remains present in political culture even when countries share democratic institutions, European membership and similar processes of modernisation. Both are contemporary European states, integrated into the European Union, with increasingly diverse societies and current debates about housing, inequality, social rights, identity and economic future. However, the way they interpret politics remains shaped by different historical trajectories. In Ireland, modern political culture has been built around independence, sovereignty, partition and the relationship with the United Kingdom.

Colonial history and the division of the island generated a political memory in which nation, border and autonomy occupy a central place. Even when current debates concern economic or social issues, the historical experience of being a small nation beside a larger neighbouring power continues to influence the way Ireland understands its position in Europe and the world. In Spain, contemporary political culture is deeply conditioned by a different experience: a civil war, a long dictatorship, a negotiated transition and a territorial model that tries to balance unity and internal diversity. This means that many Spanish political debates do not refer only to the present, but also to the interpretation of the recent past.

Democratic memory, Francoism, the monarchy, the 1978 Constitution, the autonomous state and territorial identities are part of a still sensitive political framework. Religion also shows how an apparently common element can fulfil different political functions. In Ireland, Catholicism long acted as a marker of national identity in relation to British and Protestant power, before losing much of its social authority through accelerated secularisation. In Spain, Catholicism was more closely connected to state power and Francoist national Catholicism, which explains why its public presence remains linked to debates about memory, education, public morality and institutions.

The relationship with Europe reinforces this comparison. For Ireland, Europe was a way of expanding political and economic space beyond historical dependence on the United Kingdom. For Spain, Europe represented the consolidation of democracy and a return to the Western political space after decades of dictatorship. In both cases, European integration acted as a tool of modernisation, but each country arrived in Europe from a different historical need.

Therefore, the comparison between Ireland and Spain should not be presented as an opposition between two models, one better and the other worse. Both countries have built stable democracies, experienced deep social transformations and continue to face the tensions of contemporary European societies. The main difference lies in the type of past that still gives shape to their debates. Ireland shows how colonial history, independence and partition can continue to influence a modern democracy.

Spain shows how a civil war, a dictatorship and a democratic transition can continue to condition public memory, national identity and territorial debate. In both cases, history does not determine every political decision, but it does provide the language through which each society interprets conflict. Politics, therefore, cannot be understood only as the management of the present. It is also a way of relating to the past.

Ireland and Spain share the European framework, but they do not share the same historical memory. That difference helps explain why two countries with common elements can develop such different political cultures.

## Key takeaways

1. Ireland and Spain share certain historical features, including Catholic influence, emigration, modernisation and European integration, but their political cultures come from different historical wounds.

2. Ireland's modern political culture has been strongly shaped by British rule, the struggle for independence, partition and the continuing significance of Northern Ireland.

3. Spain's contemporary political culture cannot be understood without the Civil War, Franco's dictatorship, the democratic transition and the territorial model created after 1978.

4. Catholicism played a central role in both countries, but not in the same way: in Ireland, it often marked national difference; in Spain, it became closely linked to state power, especially during Franco's regime.

5. European integration acted as a modernising force in both countries, but with different meanings: for Ireland, it widened room for manoeuvre in relation to the United Kingdom; for Spain, it confirmed democratic reintegration after dictatorship.

6. The comparison shows that political culture is shaped not only by institutions and elections, but also by memory, identity, territory and the historical narratives through which societies understand themselves.
